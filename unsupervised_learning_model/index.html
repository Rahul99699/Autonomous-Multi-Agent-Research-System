Hierarchical Clustering

Bottom-Up Approach

Start with each point as its own cluster. Iteratively merge the two closest clusters until one remains. Build dendrogram from leaves to root.

  • 1Each point = one cluster (n clusters)
  • 2Compute pairwise distance matrix
  • 3Merge two closest clusters
  • 4Update distance matrix
  • 5Repeat until 1 cluster remains

Top-Down Approach (DIANA)

Start with all points in one cluster. Recursively split into two sub-clusters using a splinter group until each point is alone. More computationally expensive.

??
Agglomerative is far more common in practice due to lower complexity O(ný log n) vs divisive O(2n).

Why Dimensionality Reduction?

The Curse of Dimensionality

As dimensions grow, data becomes increasingly sparse. Distance metrics lose meaning, models overfit, and computational cost explodes.

BENEFITS
  • Remove noise & redundancy
  • Faster training
  • Enable 2D/3D visualization
  • Prevent overfitting
TRADEOFFS
  • Information loss
  • Interpretability cost
  • Non-linear harder to explain
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity
??

Apriori vs FP-Growth

Apriori Algorithm

Uses the anti-monotone property: all subsets of a frequent itemset must also be frequent. Generates candidates level-by-level (BFS).

  • 1Find all frequent 1-itemsets (support = minSup)
  • 2Generate candidate 2-itemsets from frequent 1-itemsets
  • 3Prune infrequent candidates
  • 4Repeat until no new frequent itemsets
?? Multiple database scans required. Slow for large datasets with low support thresholds.

FP-Growth Algorithm

Builds a compact FP-Tree from the database (only 2 scans). Mines frequent patterns directly from the tree without candidate generation.

?
FP-Growth is typically 10100æ faster than Apriori. No candidate generation step. Memory-efficient with FP-Tree compression.

Anomaly Detection  Isolation Forest & LOF

Isolation Forest

Anomalies are easier to isolate  they need fewer random splits. Builds random trees; anomaly score = average path length (shorter = more anomalous). O(n log n), highly scalable.

Local Outlier Factor (LOF)

LOF(p) = avg(lrd(o)) / lrd(p)

Compares local density of a point to its neighbors. LOF >> 1 = anomaly. Context-aware  captures local density variations.

Applications
  • Credit card fraud
  • Network intrusion
  • Medical diagnosis
  • Industrial QA
Novelty vs Anomaly

Anomaly: outlier in training data. Novelty: new pattern not seen during training.

Ethical Considerations

?? Algorithmic Bias in Clustering
  • Clusters can encode historical discrimination
  • Distance metrics reflect feature choices (biased data ? biased clusters)
  • Anomaly detection may flag minorities as "outliers"
  • Market segmentation can reinforce pricing inequality

Interpretability Challenges

Unlike supervised models, unsupervised results lack ground truth. Cluster labels are arbitrary  "Cluster 2" means nothing without domain interpretation. Requires domain expert validation.

?
Best Practice: Always validate clusters against domain knowledge. Visualize, interpret, and challenge cluster assignments before acting on them.
??

Metric Deep Dive

Silhouette Score: Measures how similar a point is to its own cluster vs other clusters. Range [-1,1]. Higher is better.

Project Conclusions

? Use PCA when

You need fast preprocessing, interpretable components, or dimensionality reduction before training another model. Also ideal for explained variance analysis.

? Use t-SNE when

You need to visualize high-dimensional data and understand cluster structure. Not suitable for downstream ML tasks as distances are not meaningful globally.

? Use UMAP when

You need t-SNE quality but faster execution, or when global structure preservation matters alongside local. Increasingly preferred for production pipelines.

??
Key Takeaway: For MNIST, t-SNE achieves Silhouette ~0.85 vs PCA ~0.42 in 2D. But PCA is 1050æ faster and scales to millions of samples. Choose based on your goal: interpretability/speed (PCA) vs visual cluster quality (t-SNE/UMAP).
